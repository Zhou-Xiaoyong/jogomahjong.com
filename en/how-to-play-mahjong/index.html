How to play Mahjong: rules, tips and strategies

If you came looking for a free online Mahjong game, you can start without fear. The solitaire version is simple to understand, but when you start planning the next moves, the game gains depth. This guide teaches from scratch and also brings advanced strategies for those who already play.

Game objective

The objective is to remove all tiles from the board. To do this, you must find pairs of equal tiles that are free. When the two tiles match, they disappear and release new possibilities. The fewer moves you use to clear the board, the better your score.

In some versions, the game ends when there are no more free pairs available — even if there are still tiles on the board. That's why strategy is so important: one wrong move can block the rest of the game.

What is a free tile?

A tile is considered free when it meets two conditions at the same time:

  1. No tile on top — if any tile is covering part of it, it's stuck.
  2. At least one side is open — the left side or the right side (or both) must be free, without a tile touching it.

If a tile is stuck between two tiles or covered by another layer, it cannot be used yet. This detail is what turns Mahjong into a game of observation and patience. It's not just finding pairs — it's finding pairs in the right order.

Quick tip: tiles that are at the ends of rows or at the top of the pyramid are almost always free. Start with them.

Types of traditional Mahjong tiles

A complete Mahjong set has 144 tiles, divided into suits. In solitaire, each tile appears exactly twice (to form pairs). Knowing the suits helps recognize pairs faster:

Numbered suits (3 suits, each from 1 to 9)

  • 万 (Wàn / Characters) — tiles with Chinese number characters. They are the easiest to identify because each number has a very different design.
  • 筒 (Tǒng / Circles) — also called "dots" or "balls". Each tile has 1 to 9 circles. It's the most visual suit.
  • 索 (Suǒ / Bamboo) — tiles with bamboo designs. 1 to 9 bamboos per tile.

Winds (4 tiles)

東 (East), 南 (South), 西 (West), 北 (North). In traditional 4-player Mahjong, each player represents a wind. In solitaire, they're just another type of tile to match.

Dragons (3 tiles)

中 (Red Dragon), 發 (Green Dragon), 白 (White Dragon). They are the "three stars of luck" in Chinese tradition. In solitaire, each forms its own pair.

Flowers and Seasons (8 special tiles)

The four flowers (plum, orchid, bamboo-flower, chrysanthemum) and the four seasons (spring, summer, autumn, winter) are special tiles. In some versions of the game, all flowers match each other and all seasons match each other — even if the designs are different. In our game, each flower only matches its equal.

Game modes: which one to choose?

There's no single way to play online Mahjong. Each mode has a different rhythm and challenge. Choose what matches your moment:

Classic Solitaire Mode

The traditional mode: a board with several layers, no time, no pressure. Ideal for beginners and for those who want to relax while thinking. You play at your own pace.

Relaxing Mode

Similar to classic, but with unlimited aids: hints, automatic reshuffle when stuck and no penalties. Perfect for coffee breaks or for those learning.

Timed Mode

5-minute countdown. Each correct pair gives points and extra time. Each mistake costs time. For those who love adrenaline and want to test their thinking speed.

Blitz Mode

60 seconds to make as many pairs as possible. It's the fastest mode of all — ideal for a short game on the bus or in the supermarket line.

Pyramid Mode

The board has a pyramid shape with several staggered layers. Top tiles block the ones below. Requires more spatial vision and long-term planning.

Tower Mode

Similar to pyramid, but the board is taller and narrower, like a tower. The challenge is vertical: you need to go down layer by layer without getting stuck.

Chain Mode (Combo)

Connect pairs in quick sequence to build combos. The faster you find the next pair, the higher the point multiplier. For those who love high scores.

Slide Mode

Tiles slide across the board when others are removed. The mechanics are different: you need to predict how tiles will move to release new pairs.

Mahjong Connect

Instead of stacked tiles, all tiles are on the same layer. You connect equal tiles through a free path with up to two curves. It's the "Chinese tetris" — addictive and fast.

Memory Game

Tiles face down. You flip two at a time to find pairs. In addition to strategy, it trains memory — and you learn Chinese characters effortlessly.

Strategies for beginners

If you're starting out, these tips will help clear more boards without getting stuck:

1. Start by removing top tiles

Tiles in upper layers block all tiles below them. Whenever possible, prioritize pairs that are on top — each removed pair releases a whole new layer of possibilities.

2. Open the sides first

Long rows often block the game because tiles in the middle are stuck on both sides. Remove tiles from the ends to create space and release the center.

3. Don't click the first pair that appears

Sometimes there are three or four equal tiles on the board. Before matching two, look where the others are. Choosing the wrong pair can block the tile you'll need at the end.

4. Count how many tiles of each type exist

If there are only two tiles of one type and both are free, it's safe to match them — you won't need them later. But if there are four copies, think twice before using two of them.

5. Use hints in moderation

The hint is useful when you're tired or want to play more relaxed. But if the goal is to improve, try finding the pairs yourself before asking for help. Visual training is half the game.

6. Accept restarting

Not every random board ends in victory. Some layouts are more difficult than others. If the game gets stuck, don't get frustrated — restarting is part of the experience. Each game teaches something new.

Advanced strategies

Those who have been playing for a while can use these tactics to improve their win rate:

Think two steps ahead

Before making a move, ask yourself: "which tiles will I release with this pair?" and "can these new pairs help me?" Sometimes it's worth leaving an easy pair aside if it releases tiles that serve no purpose.

Avoid creating "islands"

An island is when a tile (or a small group of tiles) is completely surrounded by empty spaces. These tiles often don't have a pair available and end up left over at the end. Try to keep the board connected.

Identify "critical pairs"

Critical pairs are those that, if you get the order wrong, the game gets stuck. They usually appear when two equal tiles are in positions that block each other. When you see this situation, stop and think carefully before acting.

Use reshuffle strategically

If the game has reshuffle, don't save it only for the end. Sometimes it's better to reshuffle in the middle of the game, when the board is full of possibilities, than to wait until the end when only a few tiles are left.

Traditional Mahjong vs Mahjong Solitaire vs Mahjong Connect

Many people confuse the three. They are related, but very different games:

  • Traditional Mahjong — table game for 4 players, with complex rules, points, turns and group strategy. It's the original Chinese game, with centuries of history.
  • Mahjong Solitaire — single-player version, created in the 80s for computers. The objective is to match pairs of free tiles until the board is cleared. No turns or opponents.
  • Mahjong Connect — also for one player, but all tiles are on the same layer. You connect pairs through a path with up to two curves. It's faster and more visual.

In Brazil, many people use "Mahjong" to refer to all these online versions. That's fine — the important thing is to have fun.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need to know Chinese characters to play?

No! Each tile has a unique design that you recognize visually, like playing cards. After a few games you already identify them all effortlessly. And if you want to learn the characters, even better — the game teaches you without you noticing.

Does every board have a solution?

Not necessarily. Randomly generated boards may not have a solution, especially in more difficult modes. That's why most online games offer reshuffle or hints.

What's the best way to practice?

Start with relaxing or classic mode, without time pressure. When you can clear the board frequently, move to timed or more challenging modes. 10 to 15 minutes of daily practice already makes a big difference.

Is playing Mahjong good for the brain?

Yes! Mahjong exercises visual memory, attention, strategic reasoning and patience. It's a puzzle that's never the same — each game is a new challenge. For those who want to keep their mind active, it's a great option.

Can I play on mobile?

Of course! All modes on our site work in your mobile browser, no download needed. Just access and play. The screen adapts automatically to your device size.
